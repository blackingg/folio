![The God's Palm — the character held mid-air in the pillar of light, the orange tree ring below, the blue border wall on the horizon](/blog/fencing-infinity-a-wall-a-plugin-system-and-an-old-god/gods-palm-pillar.png)

The trees made it in. Twenty-four voxel variations — eight models, three color palettes — spawned by the same worker that generates the terrain, rendered as instanced meshes, collided with using arithmetic instead of a physics engine. The empty land I complained about last time is a forest now. (The raymarched clouds, ironically, did not survive contact with the performance budget — the sky runs flat backdrop clouds these days. [That confession got its own post.](https://www.whoisblxck.xyz/blog/the-sky-is-a-backdrop-deleting-my-raymarched-clouds))

Then I did what I always do after shipping something: I went for a walk in the world. And I kept walking. And nothing happened. Forever, in every direction.

That is the thing nobody tells you about procedural worlds: infinity is not a feature. **A world isn't a place until it has a coast to stop you and a lighthouse to pull you.** The last few weeks were about building both.

#### Places as plugins

Before building any specific place, I built the system for places.

The engine now has an **ExperienceManager** — a registry of zones, each one a class with a lifecycle. Cross the preload radius and `load()` runs (fetch the GLTFs, build the meshes). Cross the trigger radius and `onEnter()` fires, and the experience gets an `update(dt)` every frame while you're inside. Wander far enough away and it disposes itself to give the memory back. I should be honest about the registry's contents: the basketball court and village in there are empty test zones — flattened ground, a trigger radius, and no models, which is exactly enough to exercise the lifecycle without committing to any content. The lifecycle is built for the heavy zones those will eventually become — a portfolio page cannot load a whole village on the off chance you stroll south-west. The first experience that actually *does* something is the old god at the end of this post.

The part I care most about is where the zones are defined: one array in `worldGen.js`, a file with a strict rule — pure data and pure functions, no three.js, no DOM. The terrain worker imports it to flatten ground under each zone. The engine imports it to build the registry. The React HUD imports it to draw the map. Change one entry, and everything agrees:

```js
// worldGen.js — one entry drives the worker, the engine, and the HUD
{
  id: "gods_palm",
  label: "God's Palm",
  emoji: "🗿",
  x: 280, z: -60,
  triggerRadius: 30,
  preloadRadius: 1000,
  flattenRadius: 45,
  targetHeight: 18,
  loadingOrb: false,
  floatRadius: 8,
  treeRingRadius: 18,
  treeClearRadius: 45,
},
```

We'll get to that one.

While a heavy zone loads, a dome of mist sits over it and fades out as you approach — no pop-in, just weather clearing. Light zones opt out with `loadingOrb: false`. That opt-in/opt-out pattern is the whole philosophy: the engine and the worker stay agnostic about specific places and only honour fields a definition declares. Adding a new place should mean writing one object and one class, and so far it does.

#### A world with a coastline

The terrain generates forever, and I wanted it to. But the home map — the part with my work in it — needed an edge.

So the border is a coastline. The wall sits at a 500-unit radius warped by three sinusoids, which is enough wobble to read as geography instead of geometry:

```js
// worldGen.js — the same formula feeds the worker, the collision, and the map
const radiusAt = (theta) =>
  BORDER.radius
  + BORDER.wobble[0] * Math.sin(theta * 3 + phaseA)
  + BORDER.wobble[1] * Math.sin(theta * 5 + phaseB)
  + BORDER.wobble[2] * Math.sin(theta * 9 + phaseC)
```

Along that line stand four staggered rows of blue trees. Blue is reserved for the border — the generator recolors any random blue grove that spawns inside the map to green, so the color itself is a signal: blue means edge. There is exactly one gate, due north, and beyond it the wilds — untamed procedural terrain, no zones, no map coverage, on purpose.

The trees are the picture, not the fence. Collision is an analytic band around the coastline formula: the player's distance from the wall centreline is checked against the formula directly, so you cannot slip between trunks on a frame spike, and the gate is the only place the band lets you through. If every wall tree disappeared tomorrow, the wall would still hold.

#### The wall that wasn't there

Then I stood in the middle of the map and looked at the horizon. No wall.

Trees were only generated for high-precision chunks near the player — the right call for the random forest, where thousands of trees you can't resolve at distance are pure waste. But the wall inherited that rule, so the map's one defining edge just wasn't there until you walked most of the way to it and watched it pop into existence.

The fix leaned on the wall being deterministic. Distant low-precision chunks now emit a thinned wall — the two middle rows, every other arc step, a quarter of the density — at positions *identical* to the full wall:

```js
// Far chunks: middle rows on every other arc step only
if (isFarChunk && (iStep % 2 !== 0 || row === 0 || row === WALL_ROWS - 1)) continue
```

Because the thinned set is an exact subset of the full set, chunks splitting as you approach fill trees in between the ones already standing. Nothing shuffles, nothing pops — the coastline just sharpens. Since the worker is pure JavaScript, I could verify this headlessly in node: generate the same stretch of wall at both precisions and diff the positions. Every far tree existed in the near set, 25% density on the nose.

#### The God's Palm

East of spawn, the map now has its lighthouse.

The lore: a flat-topped plateau like an upturned palm, the worship grounds of an old god. A ring of orange trees around a bare circle, and from that circle a pillar of light. Stand in it, and the god lifts you — eight units off the ground, held there with a slow bob, set back down gently when you step out.

Almost all of it fell out of systems that already existed. The plateau is the zone-flattening code with an immodest `targetHeight`. The orange ring is a `treeRingRadius` field the worker honours the same way it honours the wall — walk a circle, place trees, stay agnostic about why. The float is one addition on top of the player's terrain snap, eased so it feels like being held rather than launched.

The pillar was the fun constraint. This scene has **no lights** — everything is shaded by custom sun-based materials, so there is no light to "turn on." The pillar is an additive-blended shader cylinder: brightest at the base, fading with height, a slow ripple drifting upward like rising air. It costs one draw call, and its preload radius is set wide enough that it exists from anywhere inside the border. Wherever you are, there is a gold line on the horizon.

The same constraint produced the best bug of the month: the loading mist over the other zones rendered pitch black, because its material was a `MeshStandardMaterial` — a lit material in a world with zero lights, faithfully reporting the total absence of light. And the plateau had a race: the ExperienceManager loads asynchronously, terrain generation does not wait, so the first-generated world had no flattening at all — you could watch the ground swell into a mesa as you approached, which was accidentally very on-theme for an old god, and still had to go.

#### Coasts and lighthouses

Three things this stretch taught me:

**Define worlds as data, grow behavior around it.** One entry in `worldGen.js` drives terrain, collision, registry, and map. Every bug in this post got easier to fix because there was exactly one place the truth lived.

**Constraints are design partners.** No lights in the scene forced a shader pillar that is cheaper and more controllable than a real light would have been. The LOD rule that broke the wall forced the subset trick that made LOD transitions invisible.

**The math is the world; the visuals are a rumor of it.** The wall holds without its trees. The god lifts you with three lines of arithmetic. Get the invisible layer right and the visible one becomes set dressing you can swap freely.

Now when I stand at spawn and turn the camera, there is a blue coast on every horizon and a gold pillar to the east. The world finally points somewhere. Next is giving you a reason to answer it — the palm needs something waiting at the top of that light.
